P. PADMAN SURASENA, CJ

At the outset, with the concurrence for the learned Counsel for all the parties, Court

amalgamated the hearing of the following cases, namely; SC/FR/356/2021,

SC/FR/358/2021, SC/FR/364/2021, SC/FR/365/2021, SC/FR/402/2021,

SC/FR/408/2021 and SC/FR/432/2021.

All Counsel agreed that it would suffice for this Court to pronounce one Judgment in

respect of all these cases since the issue agitated in all these cases is the same.

The Inspector General of Police called for applications through the RTM 1233, dated

31-01-2020, to fill the vacancies in the posts of Assistant Superintendents of Police/

woman Assistant Superintendents of Police (other than from support services and

[SC/FR/356/2021 with SC/FR/358/2021, SC/FR/364/2021, SC/FR/365/2021, SC/FR/402/2021,

SC/FR/408/2021 & SC/FR/432/2021]

Page 139 of 142 Special Tasks Force) from the Chief Inspectors of Police/ Woman Chief Inspectors of

Police who have possessed the required qualifications. The Petitioners having applied

for the said posts had become unsuccessful in securing promotions to the posts of

Assistant Superintendent of Police through this scheme.

The Respondents have filed the document marked R7 (in SC/FR/356/2021), which

contains the marks awarded by the members of the interview panel to 445 officers

who presented themselves before the said interview panel. However, the Respondents

have admitted that no appointment was made based on the said marks awarded by

the said interview panel. Moreover, the Secretary of the Public Service Commission

has admitted that the marks awarded by the said interview panel was incorrect and

therefore the Public Service Commission had adjusted the said marks. The Secretary

of the Public Service Commission, in his affidavit has explained the reasons for this

action. Subsequently the Public Service Commission has approved the aforesaid

alterations of marks.

One of the contentions of the Petitioners is that the Public Service Commission has no

authority for the aforesaid action of alteration of marks.

However, it is the Public Service Commission which was at that time the authority to

decide on the promotions. When the interview panel appointed by the Public Service

Commission had unduly awarded marks, obviously the Public Service Commission

must step in, to correct and alter such unduly awarded marks and correct the injustices

to the other applicants. Upholding the argument advanced by the Petitioners would

amount to transferring the power vested in the appointing authority by the constitution

(in this case the Public Service Commission) to a mere interview panel. Moreover, the

Public Service Commission has given reasons as to why it had done the afore-said

alterations. I am satisfied that the action of the Public Service Commission in this

instance to alter the marks was lawful. I am also of the view that any non-alteration

of erroneously awarded marks would have been illegal. Therefore, I reject this

argument advanced by the Petitioners.

Page 140 of 142 The second argument advanced by the Petitioners is that the Public Service

Commission had permitted some persons who are not members of the appointed

interview panel to assist them in the selection of some of the appointees. It is on that

basis that the Petitioners have prayed to quash the appointments of certain

Respondents who belong to the State Intelligence Service (SIS). This is because of

the fact that the interview panel had been assisted by some senior officers of the SIS.

Undoubtedly, the State Intelligence Service is an entity which is expected to conduct

covert operations without the knowledge of anyone. This position has been explained

by several Respondents and the Director General of the State Intelligence Service,

Major General Tuan Suresh Sallay in their respective affidavits.

The reason as to why the said interview panel had decided to obtain assistance from

senior officers of the SIS is because it is only those senior officers who are aware of

the functions of those officers who always perform their duties under cover.

Sometimes they are compelled to perform their duties in disguised forms / under

assumed identities / in covert capacities / under concealment of their true identities /

in an undercover capacity. They are tasked with securing intelligence for the purpose

of addressing threats to National Security of Sri Lanka. As such, these personnel

attached to the SIS deal with very sensitive information through their engagements.1

That is the very nature of their duties. The performance of these intelligence officers,

therefore cannot be made known to anyone. It has been the existing practice for

senior members of the SIS to be present in an administrative capacity to facilitate

document verification during the interview process.2 Therefore, I am unable to accept

the submissions made by the learned Counsel for the Petitioners that the evaluation

of the performance of these intelligence officers also must be done in such a manner

interviews conducted and the marks allocated to the SIS officers null and void.

presented on behalf of the Petitioners. Hence the Petitioners are not entitled to
