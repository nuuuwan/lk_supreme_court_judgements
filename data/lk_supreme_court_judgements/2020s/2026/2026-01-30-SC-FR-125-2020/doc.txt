Learned Counsel for the Petitioners and the Respondents in all five cases, namely;

SC/FR/125/2020, SC/FR/126/2020, SC/FR/127/2020, SC/FR/128/2020 and SC/FR/129/2020;

informed Court at the very outset, that Court can amalgamate all these six cases; conduct a

consolidated hearing; and then pronounce one Judgment which would be applicable to all the

five cases.

Sri Lanka Police, by the Gazette dated 12th June 2015, called for applications for recruitment

of Assistant Superintendents of Police. This recruitment was through an open competitive

examination. The Petitioners of all these applications are persons who have successfully

passed this examination. The Petitioners (in SC/FR/125/2020) have produced this gazette

notification marked P4. It was the Department of Examinations which had conducted the said

examination. After completion of the various stages of the recruitment process, such as;

interviews, medical examinations, etc., the National Police Commission by its decision taken

on 18th July 2019 had decided to recruit 18 successful candidates whose names appear on the

document marked P9. The Petitioners are persons who are immediately below the 18th

candidate mentioned in P9.

However, Sri Lanka Police had recruited only 14 candidates out of the said 18 candidates and

appointed them as Assistant Superintendents of Police with effect from 01st October 2019.

Balance four persons in the said list of 18 candidates were rejected based on the background

information that the police recruitment division had received. Thus, it is the position of the

Petitioners that they, being the persons who are next in line after the aforesaid fourteen

successful candidates, have had the legitimate expectation to be appointed next, as Assistant

Superintendents of Police.

Although, the Petitioners have stated that there is a balance of six vacancies, according to the

Petition itself, Sri Lanka Police had rejected only four candidates out of the successful 18

candidates leaving only four vacancies. However, later on, Sri Lanka Police by its decision

taken on 05th May 2019, had decided not to proceed to fill these remaining vacancies from

the Petitioners. It is this decision that the Petitioners have challenged in these applications.

I observe that Sri Lanka Police had called for applications for the appointment of Assistant

Superintendents of Police for which the closing date was set as 13th August 2015. The results

[SC/FR/125/2020, SC/FR/126/2020, SC/FR/127/2020, SC/FR/128/2020 and SC/FR/129/2020]

14 candidates were appointed as Assistant Superintendents of Police with effect from 01st

October 2019.

Petitioners sat the open competitive examination and failed to secure a place within the first

18 persons who were selected to be recruited as Assistant Superintendents of Police through

the said open competitive examination. I observe that according to P4 there are many other

qualifications that one has to fulfil, for appointment as an Assistant Superintendent of Police.

Although at one point of time, Sri Lanka Police had been attempting to appoint the Petitioners

to the remaining vacancies, since the Petitioners had not secured a place amongst the first

eighteen persons selected, I am of the view that they could not have entertained any

legitimate expectation that Sri Lanka Police would still recruit them also as Assistant

Superintendents of Police.

In my view, it is natural and it is open for the authority engaged in recruiting officers to decide

not to continue to fill the posts until the end of the persons who have been listed as successful

in a given examination. This could be for varied reasons. Thus, such candidate cannot be said

to have had a legitimate expectation that he or she should necessarily be recruited to fill those

vacancies. Indeed, that is exactly what has happened to the four persons at the tail end of

the list of 18 persons selected for appointment.

As I have already held that the Petitioners cannot have a legitimate expectation that they

should be recruited to fill those vacancies, this matter must stand necessarily disposed on that

conclusion alone. However, in any case I would like to make the following observation also in

view of the fact that the Petitioners have prayed for a direction on the Respondents to appoint

the Petitioners to the posts of Assistant Superintendents of Police with effect from 01st October

2019.

One of the qualifications for any applicant to be appointed as an Assistant Superintendent of

Police is, to be a person under 28 years of age. This was presumably as at the closing date

for submission of the applications which was 13th August 2015. Admittedly, the Petitioners are

persons born in 1988 and 1989. Thus, as at today, the Petitioners are person between 36 to

38 years of age. Therefore, the question arises as to the fitness of the Petitioners as they are

now long passed the age of persons who were to be trained for appointment as Assistant

Superintendents of Police.

Moreover, if the Petitioners are appointed as Assistant Superintendents of Police with effect

from 01-10-2019, they will have to be treated as Assistant Superintendents of Police who have

worked in that post for about six years. They may then be qualified to become Superintendents

of Police and Senior Superintendents of Police. This they would achieve without even one

day’s experience which is not at all going to be healthy for the society at large.

Court cannot turn a blind eye to this aspect of the case, particularly because Fundamental

Rights jurisdiction is one that will involve the exercise of equitable jurisdiction.

In any case, considering the present age of the Petitioners, I am unable to direct the

Respondents to necessarily recruit a person aged between 36 to 38 years and cause that

person to undergo a training, rigorous enough to prepare that person for an appointment as

an Assistant Superintendent of Police.

The restriction placed on the age of a candidate for recruitment to this post is not without any

reason. The reasons are obvious. Training of a person prior to appointment to a senior post

in Sri Lanka Police necessarily involves rigorous physical training. This is another

disqualification as to why the Petitioners cannot be appointed now as Assistant

Superintendents of Police. Accordingly, I hold that the Petitioners are not entitled to succeed

with their Petitions. I direct that the Petitions must stand dismissed without cost.
