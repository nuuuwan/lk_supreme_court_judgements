ACHALA WENGAPPULI, J.

The 1st Petitioner, the Graduate Employees’ Union, along with the 2nd to 13th

Petitioners, who are members of that trade union, have invoked the jurisdiction

of this Court conferred under Articles 17 and 126(1) of the Constitution on an

allegation of an infringement of fundamental rights by the executive and

administrative actions of the Respondents.

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

10

When this petition was supported for the consideration of leave to proceed

on 06.08.2012 by the learned President’s Counsel who appeared on behalf of the

Petitioners, this Court thought it fit to grant Leave to Proceed in respect of the

alleged violation of fundamental rights guaranteed to them under Article 12(1) of

the Constitution.

In the said application, it is stated that the 2nd to 13th Petitioners were

recruited to the Public Service under the Government Graduate Scheme Program

during the intakes of 1999, 2004 and 2009. They were so recruited upon a policy

decision taken to implement “priority activities of the Government.” They were

initially appointed as Graduate Trainees and assigned randomly to various

public entities. In 2006, with the issuance of Public Administration Circular No.

06/2006, those who have been recruited under the said Scheme were categorised

as “Associate Officers” and placed under salary scale MN-4.

The 2nd to 13th Petitioners claim that, upon representations being made to

that effect, the Department of Management Services has prepared a draft Service

Minute for these graduates, who are similarly circumstanced as the Petitioners

and to place them in a suitable salary scale. Further discussions on the said draft

Service Minute were conducted from time to time with various agencies of the

executive and when the 3rd Petitioner lodged his protest for the continued failure

on the part of the administration to publish a Service Minute on 10.09.2007, the

then Director General (Establishments) by his letter dated 04.10.2007 conveyed

that certain actions have been taken to approve the Service Minute.

Therefore, the Petitioners alleged that the Respondents, despite repeated

requests made to that effect, have failed to publish a Service Minute and made

the career prospects of those Graduate employees very bleak. In this regard, the

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

11

1st Petitioner has filed an application in this Court (SCFR 183/2009) seeking a

direction of Court on the Respondents who are named in that application to

finalise and publish a Service Minute for Graduate employees who were

recruited in the years 1999 and 2004 within a reasonable time.

When the Petitioners have learnt that the Cabinet of Ministers have

approved a Service Minute for Graduate employees on 18.01.2012, they raised

objections to the same on the basis that it failed to introduce an acceptable salary

scale and acceptable promotional prospects as there was no provision for their

eligibility to apply for Sri Lanka Administrative Service. The Petitioners expected

themselves to be placed in the salary scale of MN-5, instead of current salary

scale MN-4.

On 14.02.2012, the Ministry of Public Administration and Home Affairs

published a Service Minute titled “Minute of the Programme Officers Service” in the

Government Gazette No. 1745/11 for all Graduates, who have been recruited to

the Public Service under the Government Graduate Scheme Programs of 1994,

1999, 2005 and 2009 (P23). Once again, in the said Service Minute, they were

placed in the salary scale MN-4. The Petitioners did not accept the said Service

Minute and made representations to the relevant authorities as in terms of the

said Minute there are only grade promotions, commencing from Grade III, which

is the recruitment Grade and then to Grade II and Grade I. Since there was no

positive response, the Petitioners have made a complaint to Human Rights

Commission of Sri Lanka on 12.03.2012.

In these circumstances, the Petitioners seek inter alia a declaration that their

fundamental rights under Article 12(1) are violated by the Respondents and

“[D]eclare that the Minute of the Programme Officers Service” dated 14.02.2012, marked

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

12

as P23, is null and void and no force in law” and the additional relief of cancelling

P23. They also sought an order of Court for a direction on the Respondents to

formulate a Service Minute.

The 1st Respondent, being the Secretary to the Ministry of Public

Administration, resisted the application of the Petitioners, and denied the

allegation made by them that, in the process of formulating the Minute of the

Programme Officers Service and in granting approval thereto by the Public Service

Commission, the Respondents have infringed their fundamental rights.

In paragraphs 41(b) and (c) of the Statement of Objection, the 1st

Respondent averred that letter P22, makes it abundantly clear that the 1st

Petitioner Union and its office bearers were fully aware of the contents of the

said Minute of the Programme Officers Service by 13.02.2012 and therefore the

instant application filed has been filed out of time by the Petitioners and as such

it should be dismissed in limine.

The Petitioners, in paragraph 31 of their counter affidavit, denied the

averments contained in the 41st paragraph of the 1st Respondent’s Statement of

Objections and stated that they made two complaints to the Sri Lanka Human

Rights Commission on 12.03.2012 (P24).

When this application was taken up for hearing on 04.12.2024, learned

DSG who represented the Respondents, reiterated the objection which the 1st

Respondent has taken in his Statement of Objections to the effect that the

application of the Petitioners is time barred. It was contended by the

Respondents, that the Petitioners, in paragraph 55 of their petition have averred

that the 1st Respondent Union, by her letter dated 13.02.2012 (P22), conveyed the

objection of its membership to the 1st Respondent and other relevant authorities

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

13

on the then finalised Minute of the Programme Officers Service. Learned DSG

therefore contended that the Petitioners were fully aware of the Service Minute,

which was formulated by the Respondents to cover all Graduates who were

recruited to the Public Service under the Government Graduate Scheme Program

in the years 1994, 1999, 2005 and 2009 but have filed the instant application

before this Court out of time. Therefore, he moved that the instant application be

dismissed in limine.

Perusal of the reliefs sought by the Petitioners in the instant application

revealed that the primary relief sought from this Court by them is to nullify the

Minute of the Programme Officers Service. Paragraphs “C” and “D” specifically

framed with a view of nullifying the said Minute of the Programme Officers Service,

which was tendered along with their petition, marked as P23.

It is also evident from the narrative of the Petitioners that during the past

so many years they were continuously making representations to the relevant

authorities demanding that a Service Minute for them should be formulated.

That did eventually take place with the publication of the Minute of the

Programme Officers Service in the Government Gazette No. 1745/11 on 14.02.2012,

by the Director General of the Combined Services, Ministry of Public

Administration and Home Affairs. P23 also indicates that the Public Service

Commission has approved the said Service Minute on 21.12.2011.

The instant application was filed by the Petitioners only on 10.05.2012.

Article 126(2) sets out a time limit within which an alleged infringement of

fundamental rights should be brought before the Supreme Court, by invoking its

jurisdiction conferred under Articles 17 and 126(1). That time period is

considered as mandatory. The period of one month is obviously exceeded by the

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

14

Petitioners in filing this application. In view of the objection taken on the basis

that their application is time barred, the Petitioners heavily relied on the

document marked as P24.

The document P24 is a form issued by the Sri Lanka Human Rights

Commission setting out the format in which the complaint of any infringement

of a human right, should be lodged with that Commission. The details contained

in P24 relates to a complaint made by the Secretary of the 1st Petitioner Union,

S.C. Sooriyarachchi, the 3rd Petitioner, regarding a formulation of a Service Minute

without adopting a consultative process. The relief sought therein relates to the

salary structure implemented by P. A. Circular No. 6/2006, by which the

applicant is placed in the salary scale of MN-4. He also sought to implement a

new Service Minute. The document P24 is dated 12.03.2012. However, there is no

supporting documents to indicate that complaint was in fact lodged at the

Commission office as no receipt issued by that Commission confirming the

acceptance and the number allocated to the complaint was produced by the

Petitioners.

Along with P24, the Petitioners have tendered a receipt issued by the said

Commission indicating that his complaint was accepted on 12.03.2012, and

allocated reference No. HRC 110/2012. But the person who made that complaint

is one W.P. Sumith Jayasena and not the 3rd Petitioner, who authored P24. The

name of W.P. Sumith Jayasena appears in the caption of the Petitioners application

as the 4th Petitioner. But the said receipt does not indicate the nature of the

complaint he has lodged at the Commission. In the counter affidavit, despite the

specific objection taken in the Statement of Objections by the 1st Respondent, the

Petitioners have failed to make any reference to the said receipt issued by the

Commission or in respect of which type of a complaint it was issued. It is

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

15

probable that the 3rd and 4th Petitioners have lodged different complaints to the

said Commission, as they were office bearers of the 1st Petitioner Trade Union.

This fact is evident from the document 1R4, which is a certified copy of

proceedings of S.C. Application No. 183/2009, where the 1st Petitioner and the 3rd

Petitioner in the instant application have filed that application alleging

infringement of fundamental rights. The said application was withdrawn by the

petitioners of that application, along with the 1st and 3rd Petitioners, on

14.05.2012, after stating to Court that they reserved their right to take action

whatever they deem as necessary to get their demands. Thus, it is not that the

publication of the Minute of the Programme Officers Service was done in secrecy.

The 1st Petitioner Union was very much active in strongly maintaining the

demand of placing themselves in the salary scale of MN-5, during the

formulation of the said Service Minute and P22 indicated that the Petitioners

were fully aware that demand was not met by the Service Minute which has

been finalised by the Respondents and sent for publication.

When the 1st Respondent has specifically taken up the time bar objection

the Petitioners ought to have taken an effort to counter that objection by

adducing relevant factual matters, which they have totally neglected to. Thus,

the Petitioners cannot derive any support from the provisions contained in

Section 13 (1) of Human Rights Commission of Sri Lanka Act No. 21 of 1996 to

ward off the effect of the preliminary objection. Thus, in my view the objection

raised by the 1st Respondent, that the instant application filed by the Petitioners

is time barred, should succeed.

S.C. (FR) Application No.237/2012

16

The objection that the 1st Petitioner, being a Trade Union, has no status to

invoke jurisdiction of this Court need not be considered by this Court, in view of

the conclusion reached on the time bar objection raised by the learned DSG.

The petition of the Petitioners dated 09.05.2012 is accordingly dismissed. I

make no order as to costs.
